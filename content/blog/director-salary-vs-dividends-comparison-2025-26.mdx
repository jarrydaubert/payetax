## How Salary is Taxed (2025-26)

When you pay yourself a salary, three taxes apply:

### 1. Income Tax (Paid by You)
| Band | Rate | Income Range |
|------|------|--------------|
| Personal Allowance | 0% | £0 - £12,570 |
| Basic rate | 20% | £12,571 - £50,270 |
| Higher rate | 40% | £50,271 - £125,140 |
| Additional rate | 45% | Above £125,140 |

### 2. Employee National Insurance (Paid by You)
| Threshold | Rate |
|-----------|------|
| £12,570 - £50,270 | 8% |
| Above £50,270 | 2% |

### 3. Employer National Insurance (Paid by Company)
| Threshold | Rate |
|-----------|------|
| Above £5,000 | 15% |

**Combined marginal rates on salary:**

| Income Level | Income Tax | Employee NI | Employer NI | Total |
|--------------|------------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| £12,570 - £50,270 | 20% | 8% | 15% | 43% |
| £50,271 - £125,140 | 40% | 2% | 15% | 57% |
| Above £125,140 | 45% | 2% | 15% | 62% |

**Key insight**: At higher rate, you lose 57p of every additional £1 of salary to tax. That's before considering the company's perspective.

## How Dividends are Taxed (2025-26)

Dividends go through two layers of tax:

### 1. Corporation Tax (Paid by Company)
| Profit Level | Rate |
|--------------|------|
| Up to £50,000 | 19% |
| £50,001 - £250,000 | 19-25% (marginal relief) |
| Above £250,000 | 25% |

### 2. Dividend Tax (Paid by You)
| Band | Rate | Total Income Range |
|------|------|-------------------|
| Dividend Allowance | 0% | First £500 |
| Basic rate | 8.75% | £12,571 - £50,270 |
| Higher rate | 33.75% | £50,271 - £125,140 |
| Additional rate | 39.35% | Above £125,140 |

**Combined effective rates on dividends (assuming 19% Corporation Tax at the small profits rate):**

For every £100 of company profit paid as dividend:

| Your Tax Band | Corp Tax | Post-CT Amount | Dividend Tax | Net to You | Effective Rate |
|---------------|----------|----------------|--------------|------------|----------------|
| Basic rate | £19 | £81 | £7.09 | £73.91 | 26.1% |
| Higher rate | £19 | £81 | £27.34 | £53.66 | 46.3% |
| Additional rate | £19 | £81 | £31.87 | £49.13 | 50.9% |

**Key insight**: In the basic rate band, dividends have an effective rate of 26.1% vs 43% for salary (at the 19% CT rate). That's a 16.9 percentage point saving. At higher profits, the combined dividend rate rises because Corporation Tax is higher.

## The £12,570 Salary Strategy Explained

The most common director pay strategy is:
- **Salary**: £12,570 (Personal Allowance)
- **Rest**: Dividends

**Why £12,570?**

1. **Zero Income Tax** on salary (fully covered by Personal Allowance)
2. **Zero Employee NI** on salary (below the Primary Threshold)
3. **Minimal Employer NI**: (£12,570 - £5,000) × 15% = £1,136/year
4. **State Pension credits**: You qualify for credits at this level
5. **Dividends taxed at lower rates** than equivalent salary would be

### The Math

With a £12,570 salary:
- Income Tax: £0
- Employee NI: £0
- Employer NI: £1,136
- **Total cost to extract £12,570**: £13,706

With salary above £12,570, each additional £1 costs:
- Income Tax: 20p
- Employee NI: 8p
- Employer NI: 15p (on gross before this is added)
- **Total marginal cost**: ~43p per £1

With dividends (in basic rate band):
- Corporation Tax: 19p per £1 of profit
- Dividend Tax: 8.75% on 81p = 7.09p
- **Total cost**: 26.09p per £1

**Savings**: 43p - 26.09p = **16.91p per £1** by using dividends instead of salary above £12,570.

## When Higher Salary Makes Sense

Despite dividends being more tax-efficient, there are valid reasons to pay higher salary:

### 1. Mortgage Applications
Lenders typically:
- Use 4-5× salary for affordability calculations
- Count 100% of salary income
- Count only 50-60% of dividends (if at all)

**Example**: Need a £300,000 mortgage?
- With £12,570 salary: Lenders may cap you at £60,000 (5× salary)
- With £60,000 salary: Lenders may offer £300,000 (5× salary)

**Strategy**: Increase salary 6-12 months before applying, then revert.

### 2. Pension Contributions
To claim tax relief on personal pension contributions, you need "relevant UK earnings" (salary, not dividends).

**Annual allowance**: Up to £60,000 or 100% of relevant earnings

If your salary is £12,570, you can only get tax relief on £12,570 of personal pension contributions.

**Strategy**: If making large pension contributions, higher salary may be needed. Alternatively, make employer contributions (no limit on relevant earnings).

### 3. Maternity/Paternity Pay
Statutory Maternity Pay (SMP) is based on average weekly earnings:
- At £12,570 salary: ~£241/week average = minimum SMP
- At higher salary: Higher SMP entitlement

### 4. Employment Allowance
If your company qualifies for Employment Allowance (£10,500 in 2025-26):
- Employer NI is effectively free up to this amount
- Makes higher salaries more attractive

**Note**: Single-director companies with no other employees typically don't qualify.

## Worked Example 1: £50,000 Company Profit

Let's compare three strategies for a director with £50,000 company profit:

### Strategy A: All Salary

**Calculation:**
- Gross salary available: £50,000 - Employer NI
- Employer NI: (Salary - £5,000) × 15%
- Solving: Salary = £50,000 / 1.15 + (£5,000 × 0.15/1.15) = £44,130

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Gross salary | £44,130 |
| Employer NI | £5,870 |
| Income Tax | (£44,130 - £12,570) × 20% = £6,312 |
| Employee NI | (£44,130 - £12,570) × 8% = £2,525 |
| **Take-home** | **£35,293** |
| **Total tax** | **£14,707** |

### Strategy B: All Dividends (£0 salary)

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Profit | £50,000 |
| Corporation Tax (19%) | £9,500 |
| Dividend available | £40,500 |
| Personal Allowance (shelters dividends) | £12,570 at 0% |
| Dividend allowance | £500 at 0% |
| Taxable dividends | £27,430 |
| Dividend Tax (8.75%) | £2,400 |
| **Take-home** | **£38,100** |
| **Total tax** | **£11,900** |

### Strategy C: Optimal Mix (£12,570 salary + dividends)

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Salary | £12,570 |
| Employer NI | £1,136 |
| Income Tax on salary | £0 |
| Employee NI on salary | £0 |
| Remaining profit | £50,000 - £12,570 - £1,136 = £36,294 |
| Corporation Tax (19%) | £6,896 |
| Dividend available | £29,398 |
| Dividend allowance | £500 at 0% |
| Taxable dividends | £28,898 |
| Dividend Tax (8.75%) | £2,529 |
| **Take-home** | £12,570 + £26,870 = **£39,440** |
| **Total tax** | **£10,560** |

### £50k Comparison Summary

| Strategy | Take-Home | Total Tax | Effective Rate |
|----------|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| All Salary | £35,293 | £14,707 | 29.4% |
| All Dividends | £38,100 | £11,900 | 23.8% |
| **Optimal Mix** | **£39,440** | **£10,560** | **21.1%** |

**Winner**: Optimal mix saves **£4,147/year** vs all salary.

## Factors That Change the Calculation

### Student Loans
If you have a student loan, repayments are:
- 9% on income above threshold (Plan 1: £26,065, Plan 2: £28,470)
- Applied to salary AND dividends (via Self Assessment)

With student loans, the dividend advantage shrinks slightly because you pay loan repayments either way.

### Scottish Taxpayers
Scottish Income Tax rates differ:
- Starter rate: 19% (£12,571 - £15,397)
- Basic rate: 20% (£15,398 - £27,491)
- Intermediate rate: 21% (£27,492 - £43,662)
- Higher rate: 42% (£43,663 - £75,000)
- Advanced rate: 45% (£75,001 - £125,140)
- Top rate: 48% (above £125,140)

Scottish directors may find slightly different optimal salary levels.

### Pension Contributions
If making significant pension contributions:
- Personal contributions need "relevant earnings" (salary)
- Employer contributions have no such limit
- Both reduce Corporation Tax

For heavy pension savers, higher salary or employer contributions may be optimal.

### Other Income
If you have other income (employment, rental, investments):
- Your Personal Allowance may already be used
- You may already be in a higher tax band
- The optimal director salary changes

## The Verdict: Which Saves More Tax?

**Dividends save more tax than salary** in almost every scenario because they avoid National Insurance (both employee and employer).

**The optimal strategy** for most directors is:
1. Pay salary at the Personal Allowance (£12,570)
2. Take remaining profits as dividends
3. Adjust if you need higher salary for mortgages/pensions

**Expected savings** vs all-salary:
- £50k profit: ~£4,100/year
- £100k profit: ~£5,200/year
- £150k profit: ~£9,600/year

Over a 10-year career, that's **tens of thousands** kept in your pocket instead of going to HMRC.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is dividends always better than salary?**  
A: Dividends usually avoid NI, but a small salary can secure NI credits. The best mix depends on profit, allowances, and personal circumstances.

**Q: Do Scottish rates apply to dividends?**  
A: No. Scottish rates apply to non-savings, non-dividend income. Dividends use UK-wide rates.

**Q: Can I take dividends without profit?**  
A: Dividends must be paid from distributable profits or retained earnings. Paying dividends without reserves is illegal.
