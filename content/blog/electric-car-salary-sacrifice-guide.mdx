Electric car salary sacrifice lets you lease a brand-new EV out of your **pre-tax** salary, so you save income tax and National Insurance on the cost — and then pay only a small **benefit-in-kind (BiK)** charge on the car. For 2026-27 that BiK rate is just **4%**, which is why the arrangement remains one of the most tax-efficient benefits a UK employer can offer.

This guide explains how it works, what it does to your payslip, who benefits most, and the trade-offs worth checking before you commit. It is written for PAYE employees, with a dedicated section for company directors and one-person limited companies.

> **Freshness note (15 July 2026):** The Autumn Budget 2025 introduced a cap on the National Insurance advantage of *pension* salary sacrifice from 6 April 2029, but **left electric car schemes untouched**. Fully electric cars also remain outside the Optional Remuneration Arrangement (OpRA) restrictions that removed the tax advantage from most other salary-sacrifice perks. All rates below were checked against GOV.UK during this update.

## Quick Summary

- **Salary sacrifice** means giving up part of your gross salary in exchange for a non-cash benefit — here, an EV lease usually bundled with insurance, maintenance, servicing and tyres.
- Because the lease is paid before tax and NI, you get relief at your marginal rate: **28%** for a basic-rate taxpayer (20% tax + 8% NI) or **42%** for a higher-rate taxpayer (40% tax + 2% NI).
- In return you pay BiK tax on the car. For a pure EV in 2026-27 the taxable benefit is **4% of the list price** — far below the 17–37% charged on petrol and diesel cars.
- The tax break is strongest for **higher-rate taxpayers** and for **directors** who can run the lease through their own company.
- The main catches: it lowers your **gross** salary (affecting mortgages, statutory pay and some thresholds), you are usually locked in for the lease term, and the BiK rate rises gradually to 9% by 2029-30.

## What Is Electric Car Salary Sacrifice?

**Salary sacrifice** (sometimes called salary exchange) is an agreement to reduce your contractual gross pay in return for a benefit. Your employer runs the EV lease and deducts the cost from your salary *before* income tax and NI are calculated, so you never pay tax or NI on the sacrificed amount.

It is the same mechanism used for [pension salary sacrifice](/blog/salary-sacrifice-explained-2025-26), with one important difference: a pension contribution is not a taxable benefit, whereas a company car is. So an EV scheme adds a **benefit-in-kind charge** on top — but for electric cars that charge is deliberately kept low to encourage take-up.

The result is a car paid for largely out of money that would otherwise have gone to HMRC, plus the convenience of insurance, servicing, breakdown cover and tyres usually rolled into a single monthly figure.

## Why the Tax Works: The 4% BiK Rate

Every company car has a **taxable benefit** equal to its list price (technically its P11D value) multiplied by an "appropriate percentage" set by the car's CO₂ emissions. Zero-emission cars sit in the lowest band by a wide margin.

Here is how the pure-electric rate rises over the next few years:

| Tax year | Pure electric (0 g/km) BiK rate |
|---|---|
| 2025-26 | 3% |
| **2026-27** | **4%** |
| 2027-28 | 5% |
| 2028-29 | 7% |
| 2029-30 | 9% |

Source: [GOV.UK — appropriate percentage for company car benefits](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/company-car-benefit-the-appropriate-percentage-480-appendix-2). The rate is fixed for the whole tax year, so an EV taxed at 4% in 2026-27 stays at 4% until 5 April 2027. By comparison, petrol and diesel cars are taxed at 17–37% of list price in 2026-27, with the top band set to rise towards a 39% cap by 2029-30 — so a pure EV is taxed at a small fraction of an equivalent combustion car.

Two things make the arrangement efficient at once:

1. **Relief on the salary you give up.** The lease is paid from pre-tax pay, so you save income tax and NI at your marginal rate on every pound sacrificed.
2. **A low charge on what you get back.** The car is a taxable benefit, but at only 4% of list price the BiK cost is small.

> Fully electric cars are also **exempt from the OpRA rules**. Those rules, introduced in 2017, stripped the tax advantage from most salary-sacrifice benefits by taxing them on the higher of the benefit value or the salary given up. Ultra-low-emission cars were carved out — which is precisely why EV salary sacrifice still works when many other perks no longer do.

## Worked Example (2026-27)

Two employees, two different tax bands. The figures below use the 2026-27 rates in our calculator: a £12,570 personal allowance, 20% and 40% income tax, and employee NI of 8% up to £50,270 and 2% above it.

**Illustrative assumptions:** a gross lease cost sacrificed from salary, and a car list price used only to work out the BiK charge. Your real numbers depend on the specific car and the quote from your scheme provider.

### Basic-rate employee — £35,000 salary, £6,000 a year sacrificed

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Income tax saved (20% of £6,000) | £1,200 |
| Employee NI saved (8% of £6,000) | £480 |
| **Relief on the salary given up** | **£1,680** |
| BiK charge: £40,000 list price × 4% | £1,600 |
| Tax on the benefit (20% of £1,600) | −£320 |
| **Net annual tax saving** | **£1,360** |

The £6,000 lease effectively costs about **£4,640** after tax — roughly a 23% discount versus paying for the same lease out of net pay.

### Higher-rate employee — £70,000 salary, £7,200 a year sacrificed

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Income tax saved (40% of £7,200) | £2,880 |
| Employee NI saved (2% of £7,200) | £144 |
| **Relief on the salary given up** | **£3,024** |
| BiK charge: £45,000 list price × 4% | £1,800 |
| Tax on the benefit (40% of £1,800) | −£720 |
| **Net annual tax saving** | **£2,304** |

Here the £7,200 lease effectively costs about **£4,896** — close to a third off, because higher-rate relief is worth more than the modest BiK charge.

## Directors and One-Person Limited Companies

If you run your own limited company, you do not need a third-party "scheme" — the company can lease (or buy) the EV directly and provide it to you as a director. The mechanics differ slightly from an employee arrangement:

- **First-year allowances.** A **new and unused** fully electric car qualifies for a 100% first-year capital allowance if the company buys it outright, so the full cost can be set against profits in year one. Leased cars are treated differently (the rentals are deductible instead) — check the right route with your accountant.
- **BiK still applies.** You pay the same 4% benefit-in-kind charge personally as any other company-car driver.
- **Employer National Insurance.** The company saves employer NI on any salary given up, but pays **Class 1A NI at 15%** on the car's BiK value. On a low 4% benefit that Class 1A cost is small.
- **VAT.** Recovery on leased cars with any private use is usually restricted to 50% of the finance element; rules on servicing and purchased cars differ.

For directors weighing salary, dividends and benefits together, our [Director Intelligence tool](/tools/director-guide) and the [salary vs dividends guide](/blog/director-salary-vs-dividends-comparison-2025-26) are a useful starting point. This is general planning context, not regulated tax advice — confirm the treatment for your company with a qualified accountant.

## The Catches

Salary sacrifice is genuinely efficient, but it is not free money. Check these before signing.

- **It lowers your gross salary.** Mortgage lenders assess affordability on gross pay, and a multi-year car sacrifice can reduce your borrowing capacity. Statutory maternity, paternity and sick pay, and some death-in-service cover, can also be based on the reduced figure.
- **You are usually locked in.** The lease typically runs two to four years. Leaving your job, redundancy or long-term absence can trigger early-termination charges, though many schemes offer protection or a "lifestyle" buffer — read the terms.
- **The National Minimum Wage floor.** Your pay after sacrifice cannot drop below the National Minimum Wage for your hours, which caps how much lower earners can sacrifice.
- **The BiK rate is rising.** It moves to 5% in 2027-28 and reaches 9% by 2029-30 — still low, but worth factoring into a longer lease.
- **Running costs and road tax.** From April 2025 EVs pay Vehicle Excise Duty (road tax). Cars with a list price above **£50,000** (the zero-emission threshold from April 2026) also attract the Expensive Car Supplement for five years. On a salary-sacrifice lease these are normally included in the monthly cost — confirm what your quote covers.
- **On the horizon.** The Autumn Budget 2025 announced a per-mile **electric vehicle excise duty (eVED)** from **April 2028** (around 3p per mile for EVs). It does not change the salary-sacrifice tax treatment, but it is a future running cost to keep in mind.

Because sacrifice reduces gross pay, it can also nudge you under (or over) key thresholds — see our guides to the [£100k tax trap](/blog/100k-tax-trap-avoid-60-percent-tax-2025) and [student loan repayments](/blog/student-loan-repayment-changes-2025-26) if either applies to you.

## How to Set It Up

### Step 1: Check your employer offers a scheme

EV salary sacrifice needs your employer to run it. Ask HR or payroll whether a scheme exists and which provider they use. Many mid-to-large employers now offer one; smaller firms may need to set one up.

### Step 2: Get a quote and confirm what's included

Ask for the **gross** monthly sacrifice and exactly what it covers — insurance, servicing, maintenance, tyres, breakdown cover and road tax are commonly bundled. Confirm the car's list price, as that drives your BiK charge.

### Step 3: Model the payslip impact

Work out the relief on the salary given up, then subtract the BiK tax (list price × 4% × your tax rate). Enter your reduced gross salary in our [take-home pay calculator](/#tax-calculator) to see the change to your net pay.

### Step 4: Read the agreement before you sign

This is a contractual change to your salary. Check the lease term, early-termination protection, mileage limits, and what happens if you leave or go on extended leave. Keep a copy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is electric car salary sacrifice affected by the 2025 Budget pension changes?**

No. The Autumn Budget 2025 capped the National Insurance advantage on *pension* salary sacrifice above £2,000 a year from 6 April 2029. It made no change to electric car salary sacrifice, which continues under the existing rules.

**How much can I actually save?**

It depends on your tax band and the car. Relief on the sacrificed salary is worth about 28% for a basic-rate taxpayer and 42% for a higher-rate taxpayer, less the small BiK charge. In our worked examples that netted roughly £1,360 and £2,304 a year respectively. Always model your own quote.

**What happens if I leave my job?**

Your salary-sacrifice agreement ends with your employment, and the car usually goes back. Depending on the scheme this can mean an early-termination charge, so check whether your provider includes redundancy or resignation protection.

**Does it affect my mortgage application?**

It can. Lenders assess affordability on your gross salary, which salary sacrifice reduces. If you are applying for a mortgage soon, factor this in — or time the arrangement around it.

**Is an electric car better than a petrol or hybrid for this?**

For tax, yes, by a wide margin. A pure EV is taxed at 4% of list price in 2026-27, versus 17–37% for petrol and diesel. Plug-in hybrids sit in between and their rates are tightening, so the electric advantage is largest.

**Does the PayeTax calculator include company-car tax?**

No. The calculator models PAYE income tax and National Insurance, not benefit-in-kind. It can show how a lower gross salary changes your take-home pay, which is the salary-reduction half of the calculation — you then add the BiK tax separately.

## Key Takeaways

- Electric car salary sacrifice remains highly tax-efficient in 2026-27 thanks to the **4% BiK rate** and full income tax and NI relief on the salary given up.
- **Higher-rate taxpayers and directors** benefit most, but basic-rate employees still save meaningfully.
- The main trade-offs are a lower gross salary, a multi-year commitment, and a BiK rate that climbs to 9% by 2029-30.
- The 2025 Budget pension changes and the OpRA rules both leave EV schemes intact.
- Model your own quote — the exact saving depends on the car's list price and what the lease includes.
