## The UK Tax System: Core Components

The UK operates a **progressive tax system** where you pay higher rates as your income increases. Here are the main taxes you'll encounter:

### 1. Income Tax 💷
Tax on your salary, bonuses, and other income sources.

### 2. National Insurance 🛡️
Contributions that fund the NHS, state pension, and unemployment benefits.

### 3. Council Tax 🏠
Local tax for services like refuse collection and libraries (property-based).

### 4. VAT 🛒
Value Added Tax included in most purchases (you don't handle this directly as an employee).

**For most employees, Income Tax and National Insurance are the big ones** - they're automatically deducted from your paycheck through the PAYE system.

---

## Income Tax Explained: The Foundation

Income Tax is charged on your **total income above your tax-free allowance**. Here's how it works:

### Personal Allowance (Tax-Free Income)
**£12,570 for 2024-25** - This is how much you can earn without paying any Income Tax.

### Tax Rates for 2024-25

| Income Band | Rate | Tax on |
|-------------|------|--------|
| £0 - £12,570 | **0%** | Personal Allowance (tax-free) |
| £12,571 - £50,270 | **20%** | Basic Rate |
| £50,271 - £125,140 | **40%** | Higher Rate |
| £125,141+ | **45%** | Additional Rate |

### Real Example
If you earn **£35,000 per year**:
- First £12,570: **£0 tax** (personal allowance)
- Remaining £22,430: **£4,486 tax** (20% basic rate)
- **Total Income Tax: £4,486**

## National Insurance: Your Social Security Contributions

National Insurance (NI) is separate from Income Tax and has different thresholds:

### Employee National Insurance Rates 2024-25

| Weekly Earnings | Rate |
|-----------------|------|
| Up to £242 | **0%** |
| £242 - £967 | **12%** |
| Over £967 | **2%** |

### Annual Thresholds
- **Lower Earnings Limit**: £6,396 (no NI paid)
- **Primary Threshold**: £12,570 (12% rate starts)
- **Upper Earnings Limit**: £50,270 (2% rate above this)

### What National Insurance Covers
- **NHS healthcare** (free at point of use)
- **State Pension** (you need 35+ years of contributions for full pension)
- **Jobseeker's Allowance** and other benefits
- **Statutory Sick Pay** and maternity benefits

## PAYE: How Most People Pay Tax

**Pay As You Earn (PAYE)** is the system that automatically deducts tax and National Insurance from your salary before you receive it.

### How PAYE Works
1. **Your employer receives a tax code** from HMRC
2. **Tax and NI are calculated** on each payday
3. **Deductions are sent to HMRC** monthly by your employer
4. **You receive your net (take-home) pay**

### Benefits of PAYE
- ✅ **Automatic** - no tax returns needed for most people
- ✅ **Spreads payments** evenly throughout the year
- ✅ **Employer handles everything** - less admin for you

### PAYE Limitations
- ❌ **Less control** over when you pay tax
- ❌ **Can overpay** if you have multiple income sources
- ❌ **Doesn't account for all deductions** automatically

## Understanding Your Tax Code

Your **tax code** determines how much tax-free income you get each month. Here's how to read it:

### Common Tax Codes

| Code | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **1257L** | Standard personal allowance (£12,570) | Most employees |
| **1257M** | Marriage Allowance received | +£1,260 allowance |
| **1257N** | Marriage Allowance given away | -£1,260 allowance |
| **K codes** | You owe money (negative allowance) | Company car, benefits |
| **0T** | No personal allowance | High earners (£100k+) |
| **BR** | Basic rate on all income | Second job |
| **D0** | Higher rate on all income | Second job, high earner |

### How Tax Codes Work
**Example: 1257L**
- **1257** = £12,570 personal allowance ÷ 10
- **L** = Standard allowance, no adjustments

### When Tax Codes Go Wrong
❌ **Wrong code = wrong tax deducted**
- Too high: You pay too little (HMRC will want it back)
- Too low: You overpay (but get a refund)

**Always check your tax code** matches your circumstances!

## Tax Allowances and Deductions

Beyond the personal allowance, there are several ways to reduce your tax bill:

### Marriage Allowance
- **Transfer £1,260** of unused personal allowance to your spouse
- **Save up to £252** if your partner is a basic rate taxpayer
- **Available if** one partner earns under £12,570, the other under £50,270

### Pension Contributions
- **20% tax relief** automatically applied to workplace pensions
- **Higher rate relief** (40%/45%) can be claimed back
- **Annual allowance**: £60,000 (2024-25)

### Professional Development
- **Training courses** for your current job
- **Professional subscriptions** (e.g., engineering institutions)
- **Work equipment** you buy yourself

### Travel and Expenses
- **Business mileage**: 45p per mile (first 10,000 miles)
- **Working from home**: £6 per week tax-free
- **Tools and equipment** for your trade

## When You Need to File a Tax Return

Most PAYE employees **don't need to file a tax return**, but you must if you:

### Mandatory Filing
- **Earn over £100,000** (personal allowance reduces)
- **Have untaxed income over £1,000** (rental, freelance work)
- **Receive benefits** from your employer
- **Are company director**
- **Live abroad** but earn UK income

### Optional Filing (But Often Beneficial)
- **Multiple jobs** where tax codes might be wrong
- **Significant pension contributions** (to claim higher rate relief)
- **Professional expenses** not covered by employer
- **Made losses** you want to carry forward

### Self Assessment Deadlines
- **Paper return**: 31 October
- **Online return**: 31 January
- **Payment deadline**: 31 January

**Late filing penalty**: £100 immediately, then escalating fines.

## Common Tax Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

### 1. Ignoring Your Tax Code
**Problem**: Wrong tax code = wrong tax paid  
**Solution**: Check your tax code annually, especially after job changes

### 2. Not Claiming Expenses
**Problem**: Missing out on tax relief you're entitled to  
**Solution**: Keep records of work-related expenses

### 3. Emergency Tax Codes
**Problem**: Starting new job with temporary high tax rate  
**Solution**: Provide P45 from previous employer immediately

### 4. Forgotten Income
**Problem**: HMRC discovers undeclared income later  
**Solution**: Declare all income sources, however small

### 5. Marriage Allowance Oversight
**Problem**: Missing £252 annual saving  
**Solution**: Apply if one partner doesn't use full personal allowance

## Student Loans: The Hidden Tax

Student loan repayments work like tax, but aren't actually tax. Here's what you need to know:

### Repayment Rates 2024-25

| Plan Type | Threshold | Rate |
|-----------|-----------|------|
| **Plan 1** | £22,015 | 9% |
| **Plan 2** | £27,295 | 9% |
| **Plan 4** (Scotland) | £31,395 | 9% |
| **Postgraduate** | £21,000 | 6% |

### Combined Effect
If you're on Plan 2 earning £35,000:
- **Income Tax**: 20% on income above £12,570
- **National Insurance**: 12% on income above £12,570  
- **Student Loan**: 9% on income above £27,295
- **Effective rate**: Can reach 41% on some income bands

## Special Situations

### Starting Your First Job
1. **Get a National Insurance number** (if you don't have one)
2. **Provide P45** from previous employer (or complete starter checklist)
3. **Check your first payslip** for correct tax code
4. **Set up workplace pension** (auto-enrollment after 3 months)

### Changing Jobs
1. **Get P45** from old employer
2. **Give P45** to new employer immediately
3. **Check tax code** transfers correctly
4. **Consider pension transfers**

### Multiple Jobs
1. **Main job gets personal allowance** (usually)
2. **Second job taxed at basic rate** (BR code)
3. **May need tax return** to sort out overpayments
4. **National Insurance** calculated separately for each job

### Earning Over £100,000
1. **Personal allowance reduces** by £1 for every £2 over £100k
2. **Effective tax rate 60%** between £100k-£125k
3. **Consider pension contributions** to stay under threshold
4. **Must file tax return**
