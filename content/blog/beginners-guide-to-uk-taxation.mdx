New to UK tax? Look, it's not rocket science—but HMRC doesn't make it easy either. You're about to start paying tax, and you've got questions. Good. This guide gives you everything you actually need to know, without the jargon or the fluff.

## Why You Need to Understand This Stuff

Every working person in the UK pays tax. That's not news. What most people don't know? They're probably overpaying. Here's what understanding the system actually gets you:

- **More money in your pocket** through legitimate tax planning
- **Fewer nasty surprises** when HMRC comes knocking
- **Better financial decisions** when you're negotiating salary or planning retirement
- **Peace of mind** that you're not leaving money on the table

Let's break it down.

## The Two Big Ones: Income Tax and National Insurance

The UK has loads of taxes, but for most employees, two matter: Income Tax and National Insurance (NI). They're both deducted from your paycheck before you see it. Let's tackle them one at a time.

### Income Tax: What You Pay on Earnings

Income Tax is charged on what you earn above your tax-free allowance. That's £12,570 for 2024-25—anything you make below that? HMRC doesn't touch it.

Above that threshold, you pay in bands:

**Personal Allowance:** £0-£12,570 = 0% (tax-free)
**Basic Rate:** £12,571-£50,270 = 20%
**Higher Rate:** £50,271-£125,140 = 40%
**Additional Rate:** £125,141+ = 45%

#### Real Example

You earn £35,000. Here's what you pay:

- First £12,570: £0 (personal allowance)
- Remaining £22,430: £4,486 (20% basic rate)
- **Total Income Tax: £4,486 per year**

That's £374 per month. Not exactly pocket change.

### National Insurance: Your Social Security Contribution

National Insurance is separate from Income Tax. Different thresholds, different rates. It funds the NHS, your state pension, and unemployment benefits.

**Up to £12,570 per year:** 0%
**£12,570-£50,270:** 12%
**Over £50,270:** 2%

NI kicks in at the same point as Income Tax (£12,570), but the rates are different. On that £35,000 salary? You'll pay £2,692 in NI annually.

Combined? You're paying £7,178 per year in tax and NI. Your take-home is £27,822—or £2,319 per month.

### What National Insurance Actually Covers

- **NHS healthcare** (free at point of use)
- **State pension** (need 35 years of contributions for the full amount)
- **Jobseeker's Allowance** and other benefits
- **Statutory sick pay** and maternity leave

Think of it as mandatory insurance. You might never claim unemployment, but if you do? You've already paid for it.

## PAYE: How Your Tax Gets Deducted

Most employees pay tax through **Pay As You Earn (PAYE)**. Your employer deducts tax and NI from your paycheck each month and sends it to HMRC. You never see that money—it's gone before it hits your account.

### How PAYE Works

1. HMRC sends your employer a tax code
2. Your employer calculates tax and NI based on that code
3. Deductions come out of your gross pay each month
4. You get your net (take-home) pay
5. Your employer sends the tax to HMRC

No forms, no bank transfers, no drama. For most people, PAYE means you'll never need to file a tax return.

### The Upsides

- Automatic—you don't lift a finger
- Spreads payments across the year (no massive lump sum in January)
- Employer handles the paperwork

### The Downsides

- Less control over when you pay
- Easy to overpay if you've got multiple jobs or changing circumstances
- Doesn't automatically account for all deductions you're entitled to

## Tax Codes: The Secret Language

Your tax code tells your employer how much of your salary is tax-free each month. Get it wrong and you're either overpaying (losing hundreds) or underpaying (HMRC will want it back later).

### Common Tax Codes

**1257L:** Standard code for 2024-25. You get the full £12,570 personal allowance.

**1257M:** You've received Marriage Allowance from your spouse. Extra £1,260 tax-free (saves you £252 annually).

**1257N:** You've transferred Marriage Allowance to your partner. Your allowance drops to £11,310.

**K codes:** You owe HMRC money. Maybe you've got a company car, or unpaid tax from previous years. They're collecting it through your wages.

**0T:** No personal allowance. Common if you earn over £125,140, or you're on emergency tax temporarily.

**BR:** All your income from this source is taxed at 20%. Usually a second job or pension.

**D0/D1:** All income taxed at 40% or 45%. For higher earners with multiple income sources.

### How Tax Codes Work

Take 1257L:

- **1257** = Your personal allowance is £12,570 (divide by 10)
- **L** = You get the standard tax-free allowance

If your code's wrong, check it. Seriously. People lose hundreds every year because they bin the HMRC coding notice without reading it.

### When to Check Your Code

- Starting a new job
- Getting a pay rise that pushes you into a new tax band
- Taking a second job
- Starting to receive benefits (company car, medical insurance)
- Moving between England and Scotland

You can check and update your tax code through your [Personal Tax Account](https://www.gov.uk/personal-tax-account).

## Tax Allowances and Deductions

Beyond the personal allowance, there are other ways to reduce your tax bill. Legally.

### Marriage Allowance

One partner earns under £12,570? The other earns under £50,270? You can transfer £1,260 of the unused allowance to your spouse, saving up to £252 per year.

It's free money. Apply once through HMRC, and it renews automatically. You can even backdate it up to four years.

### Pension Contributions

Every pound you put into a workplace pension gets 20% tax relief automatically. Higher-rate taxpayers (40%) can claim back an additional 20% through their tax return.

**Example:** You earn £60,000. You put £5,000 into your pension via salary sacrifice.

- Your taxable income drops to £55,000
- You save £2,000 in tax (40% of £5,000)
- You also save £600 in NI (12% of £5,000)

That's £2,600 saved for a £5,000 contribution. Your actual cost? £2,400.

### Work Expenses

You can claim tax relief on things you buy for work:

- **Business mileage:** 45p per mile for the first 10,000 miles (25p after that)
- **Working from home:** £6 per week tax-free, no receipts needed
- **Professional subscriptions:** If your job requires membership of a professional body
- **Tools and equipment:** If you're a tradesperson and buy your own gear

Claim through your Personal Tax Account or via Self Assessment if you file one.

## When You Need to File a Tax Return

Most PAYE employees don't need to file a tax return. But you must if you:

- Earn over £100,000
- Have untaxed income over £1,000 (freelance work, rental income, dividends)
- Are a company director
- Get income from abroad
- Want to claim tax relief on pension contributions or expenses

### Self Assessment Deadlines

- **Paper return:** 31 October
- **Online return:** 31 January
- **Payment deadline:** 31 January

Miss the deadline? £100 penalty immediately, then escalating fines. Don't mess around with this.

## Common Tax Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

### 1. Ignoring Your Tax Code

Your employer uses the code HMRC sends them. If that code's wrong, you're paying the wrong amount of tax. Check it every April when the new tax year starts.

### 2. Not Claiming Expenses

You're entitled to claim work-related expenses. Most people don't bother. That's leaving money on the table.

### 3. Emergency Tax

Start a new job without providing your P45? You'll be stuck on emergency tax—usually overpaying significantly. Give your employer your P45 the day you start.

### 4. Forgetting About Income

Got a side hustle? Rental property? Dividends from shares? HMRC wants to know. Declare it. They'll find out eventually, and the penalties hurt.

### 5. Missing Marriage Allowance

Couples leave £252 on the table every year because they don't apply. Takes five minutes online.

## Student Loans: The Extra Tax Nobody Calls a Tax

If you've got a student loan, you're paying it back through PAYE. It works like tax, but it's not technically tax (thanks for the clarity, HMRC).

### Repayment Rates 2024-25

**Plan 1:** 9% on income over £22,015
**Plan 2:** 9% on income over £27,295
**Plan 4 (Scotland):** 9% on income over £31,395
**Postgraduate:** 6% on income over £21,000

### Combined Effect

Earn £35,000 on Plan 2?

- Income Tax: 20% on income above £12,570
- National Insurance: 12% on income above £12,570
- Student Loan: 9% on income above £27,295

On the band between £27,295 and £35,000, you're paying an effective rate of 41%. That's brutal.

## Tax Planning Strategies That Actually Work

### 1. Max Out Your Pension

Employer matching is free money. Always contribute at least enough to get the full match. If you're a higher-rate taxpayer, consider contributing more—you're getting 40% relief.

### 2. Use Your ISA Allowance

You can put £20,000 per year into an ISA (2024-25). Growth is tax-free. Income is tax-free. No capital gains tax when you sell.

### 3. Apply for Marriage Allowance

If eligible, apply. It's £252 per year, every year. Over a lifetime? That's thousands.

### 4. Keep Expense Records

Business mileage, work-from-home costs, professional subscriptions. Track them. Claim them.

### 5. Plan Around Tax Thresholds

Earning just over £100,000? Your personal allowance starts disappearing—£1 for every £2 you earn over £100k. That creates an effective 60% tax rate on income between £100k-£125,140.

Consider pension contributions to bring your income back under £100k. You'll save thousands.

## Special Situations

### Starting Your First Job

1. Get your National Insurance number (if you don't have one)
2. Provide your P45 from any previous job (or complete the starter checklist)
3. Check your first payslip to make sure your tax code's correct
4. Sign up for workplace pension auto-enrollment (happens after 3 months)

### Changing Jobs

1. Get your P45 from your old employer
2. Give it to your new employer on day one
3. Check your tax code transfers correctly
4. Consider what to do with old pension pots

### Multiple Jobs

Your main job gets your personal allowance (usually). Your second job should be taxed at basic rate (BR code). Sometimes employers mess this up—both try to give you the allowance, you underpay tax, HMRC wants it back later.

Check your codes. Make sure only one job is using your allowance.

### Earning Over £100,000

Your personal allowance reduces by £1 for every £2 you earn over £100k. At £125,140, it's gone entirely.

This creates an effective 60% tax rate on income between £100k-£125k:

- 40% income tax
- 20% from losing your allowance

You must file a Self Assessment. Consider serious pension contributions to reduce your taxable income.
