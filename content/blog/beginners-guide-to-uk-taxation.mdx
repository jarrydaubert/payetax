Starting your first job or moving to the UK can be overwhelming, especially when it comes to understanding the tax system. This guide breaks down everything you need to know about UK taxation in simple terms.

## Overview of UK Taxes

The UK tax system might seem complex at first, but it's based on a few key principles. As a UK resident, you'll primarily deal with:

- **Income Tax**: Tax on your earnings
- **National Insurance**: Contributions for state benefits
- **Council Tax**: Local services tax (if you own or rent property)
- **VAT**: Value Added Tax on goods and services (already included in prices)

## Income Tax Basics

### How Income Tax Works

Income Tax is paid on most types of income, including:
- Wages from employment
- Profits from self-employment
- Pension income
- Rental income
- Interest from savings
- Dividends from investments

### Tax-Free Personal Allowance

Everyone in the UK gets a Personal Allowance - an amount you can earn before paying tax. For 2024/25, this is **£12,570**.

**Example**: If you earn £30,000 per year:
- First £12,570: No tax
- Remaining £17,430: Taxed at 20%
- Annual tax: £3,486

### Income Tax Rates for 2024/25

| Tax Band | Taxable Income | Tax Rate |
|----------|----------------|----------|
| Personal Allowance | Up to £12,570 | 0% |
| Basic rate | £12,571 to £50,270 | 20% |
| Higher rate | £50,271 to £125,140 | 40% |
| Additional rate | Over £125,140 | 45% |

> **Note**: Scotland has different tax rates and bands.

## National Insurance Contributions (NICs)

National Insurance is a separate tax that funds state benefits like the State Pension and NHS.

### Employee National Insurance Rates (2024/25)

- **0%** on earnings up to £242 per week
- **8%** on earnings from £242 to £967 per week
- **2%** on earnings above £967 per week

### Key Points About NICs
- Paid by employees aged 16 to State Pension age
- Self-employed people pay different rates
- No NICs on pension income

## PAYE (Pay As You Earn)

If you're employed, your employer uses PAYE to deduct tax and National Insurance from your wages automatically.

### What Appears on Your Payslip
- **Gross pay**: Total before deductions
- **Income Tax**: Based on your tax code
- **National Insurance**: Based on your earnings
- **Net pay**: Take-home amount

Your employer sends the tax and NICs directly to HMRC, so you don't need to do anything.

## Self Assessment

You might need to complete a Self Assessment tax return if you:
- Are self-employed
- Earn over £100,000
- Have rental income
- Receive income from investments
- Have complex tax affairs

### Key Self Assessment Dates
- **6 April**: Tax year starts
- **5 April**: Tax year ends
- **31 October**: Paper return deadline
- **31 January**: Online return deadline and payment due

## Tax Codes Explained

Your tax code tells your employer how much tax to deduct. The most common is **1257L**, meaning you get the standard Personal Allowance.

### Understanding Your Tax Code
- Numbers = Your tax-free amount divided by 10
- Letters = Your circumstances

**Common codes**:
- **L**: Standard Personal Allowance
- **BR**: Basic rate tax on all income
- **0T**: No Personal Allowance applied

## Other Important Allowances

### Marriage Allowance
- Transfer 10% of your Personal Allowance to your spouse
- Can save up to £260 per year
- Available if one earns below £12,570

### Savings Allowances
- Basic rate taxpayers: £1,000 tax-free interest
- Higher rate taxpayers: £500 tax-free interest
- ISAs: Completely tax-free savings

### Dividend Allowance
- First £500 of dividends tax-free (2024/25)
- Rates above this depend on your tax band

## Common Tax Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not checking your tax code**: Wrong codes mean wrong tax
2. **Missing Self Assessment deadlines**: Leads to penalties
3. **Not claiming entitled allowances**: You could pay less tax
4. **Forgetting to update HMRC**: When circumstances change

## Getting Help with Taxes

### Free Resources
- **GOV.UK**: Official government guidance
- **HMRC helpline**: 0300 200 3300
- **Citizens Advice**: Free tax help
- **Tax Aid**: Charity for low-income taxpayers

### When to Get Professional Help
Consider an accountant if you:
- Are self-employed with complex accounts
- Have multiple income sources
- Own rental properties
- Need to claim significant expenses
