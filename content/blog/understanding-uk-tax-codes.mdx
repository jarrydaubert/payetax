If you're employed or receive a pension in the UK, you'll have a tax code that tells your employer or pension provider how much tax to deduct from your pay. Understanding your tax code is crucial for ensuring you pay the right amount of tax.

## What is a Tax Code?

A tax code is used by employers and pension providers to calculate how much Income Tax to take from your pay or pension. HM Revenue and Customs (HMRC) tells them which code to use to collect the right tax.

Your tax code is usually made up of numbers and letters. For example, **1257L** is the most common tax code for the 2024/25 tax year.

## How Tax Codes Work

### The Numbers
The numbers in your tax code tell your employer or pension provider how much tax-free income you get in that tax year. For example:
- **1257L**: You get the standard Personal Allowance of £12,570
- **1100L**: Your Personal Allowance has been reduced to £11,000

To work out how much tax-free pay you're entitled to, multiply the number in your tax code by 10.

### The Letters
The letters in your tax code refer to your situation and how it affects your Personal Allowance:

- **L**: You're entitled to the standard tax-free Personal Allowance
- **M**: You've received 10% of your partner's Personal Allowance
- **N**: You've transferred 10% of your Personal Allowance to your partner
- **T**: Your tax code includes other calculations to work out your Personal Allowance
- **0T**: Your Personal Allowance has been used up, or you've started a new job and your employer doesn't have the details they need
- **BR**: All your income from this job or pension is taxed at the basic rate (20%)
- **D0**: All your income from this job or pension is taxed at the higher rate (40%)
- **D1**: All your income from this job or pension is taxed at the additional rate (45%)
- **NT**: You're not paying any tax on this income

## Common Tax Codes Explained

### 1257L - The Standard Code
This is the most common tax code for employees who have one job, no untaxed income, unpaid tax, or taxable benefits. The numbers 1257 mean your tax-free allowance is £12,570.

### BR - Basic Rate
This code is often used if you have a second job or pension. All your income from this source is taxed at 20%, with no tax-free allowance applied.

### Emergency Tax Codes
If HMRC doesn't have enough information about your income, you might be put on an emergency tax code. Common emergency codes include:
- **1257L W1/M1**: Emergency tax code operated on a week 1/month 1 basis
- **BR X**: Emergency basic rate code

## What Affects Your Tax Code?

Several factors can change your tax code:

1. **Company benefits**: Company car, medical insurance, or other benefits
2. **State benefits**: Some state benefits are taxable
3. **Other income**: Rental income, part-time work, or investments
4. **Unpaid tax**: Tax owed from previous years
5. **Marriage Allowance**: Transferring Personal Allowance to/from your spouse

## How to Check Your Tax Code

You can find your tax code:
- On your payslip
- On a PAYE Coding Notice from HMRC
- In your Personal Tax Account on GOV.UK
- On a P45 form from your previous employer

## What to Do If Your Tax Code Is Wrong

If you think your tax code is wrong:

1. **Check your Personal Tax Account** on GOV.UK
2. **Contact HMRC** on 0300 200 3300
3. **Provide evidence** of your circumstances
4. **Keep records** of all correspondence

Remember, an incorrect tax code could mean you're paying too much or too little tax, so it's important to get it corrected quickly.

## Tax Code Changes

Your tax code can change during the year if:
- You start or stop receiving benefits from your job
- You start or stop receiving the State Pension
- Your income changes
- You claim Marriage Allowance

HMRC will usually send you a PAYE Coding Notice explaining any changes.
