The **Spring Statement** (sometimes called the Spring Forecast) is the Chancellor's mid-year economic update. The government has committed to one major fiscal event a year at the Budget, so the Spring Forecast is expected to be an interim update rather than a full package of tax changes.

Here's what we know, what we expect, and how to prepare.

## Quick Summary

- What the Spring Statement is and how it differs from the Autumn Budget.
- The policy areas most likely to see updates in 2026.
- How to prepare if you are affected by announced changes.

## When Is the Spring Statement 2026?

The Chancellor has asked the Office for Budget Responsibility to publish the Spring Forecast on **3 March 2026**. The government will respond with a statement to Parliament on the day.

**Key dates for your diary:**
- **Spring Forecast / Statement**: 3 March 2026
- **New tax year starts**: 6 April 2026
- **Any changes effective**: Usually 6 April 2026 onwards

---

## What Is the Spring Statement?

The Spring Statement (Spring Forecast) is the government's response to the Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR) economic forecast. It's an opportunity for the Chancellor to:

1. Update economic projections
2. Announce minor tax changes
3. Signal policy direction
4. Respond to economic conditions

**How it differs from the Autumn Budget:**
- Typically smaller in scope
- Fewer major tax announcements
- More focused on economic outlook
- Changes usually less dramatic

However, in recent years, Spring Statements have occasionally included significant tax measures—so it pays to be prepared.

## What Could Change in Spring 2026?

Based on current economic conditions and policy signals, here are areas to watch:

### Likely to Be Discussed

**1. Frozen Tax Thresholds**

The Personal Allowance (£12,570), basic rate band (£37,700), and higher‑rate threshold (£50,270) are legislated to remain frozen until April 2028.

- **Watch for**: Any change to the planned end date
- **Impact**: Frozen thresholds mean more people pay higher rates as wages rise
- **Our view**: Unlikely to change, but any movement here would be significant

[Read about frozen thresholds and fiscal drag](/blog/frozen-tax-thresholds-stealth-tax-2026)

**2. National Insurance Rates**

The main employee NI rate was cut to 8% from April 2024.

- **Watch for**: Any adjustments to employee NI rates
- **Impact**: Direct effect on take-home pay
- **Our view**: Further cuts are less likely, but still possible

**3. Fuel Duty**

The 5p fuel duty cut introduced in March 2022 has been extended and is currently set to end on 22 March 2026.

- **Watch for**: Extension or end of the cut
- **Impact**: Affects transport costs, inflation
- **Our view**: Political pressure could mean another extension

### Possible Announcements

**4. ISA Updates**

ISAs are a frequent policy target, but there are no confirmed changes yet:

- **Watch for**: Any announcement on ISA limits or simplification
- **Impact**: Changes would affect savings and investment planning
- **Our view**: If anything happens, expect incremental tweaks rather than a full redesign

**5. Pension Tax Relief**

Always under review at fiscal events:

- **Watch for**: Changes to relief rates or allowances
- **Impact**: Could affect higher earners significantly
- **Our view**: Major changes unlikely mid-cycle, but watch for signals

**6. Business Rates**

Often adjusted at fiscal events:

- **Watch for**: Relief extensions for retail/hospitality
- **Impact**: Indirect—affects prices and employment
- **Our view**: Likely some sector support

### Unlikely but Possible

**7. Income Tax Rates**

Major rate changes are rare at Spring Statements:

- **Watch for**: Signals for Autumn Budget
- **Impact**: Would be significant if announced
- **Our view**: Very unlikely without major economic shift

## How Past Spring Statements Affected Taxes

| Year | Key Announcement | Impact |
|------|------------------|--------|
| 2024 | Employee NI main rate cut to 8% from April 2024 | Lower employee NI deductions |

As you can see, while smaller than Autumn Budgets, Spring Statements can deliver meaningful changes.

## Spring Statement vs. Autumn Budget

| Feature | Spring Statement / Spring Forecast | Autumn Budget |
|---------|-----------------------------------|---------------|
| Timing | 3 March 2026 | Typically autumn |
| Scope | Interim update (not a full fiscal event) | Major fiscal event |
| Tax changes | Usually limited | Major changes possible |
| Spending | Updates only | Full review |
| OBR forecast | Published in spring; government responds by statement | Full forecast informs Budget |

For major tax planning, the Autumn Budget remains the key event. But the Spring Statement can still surprise.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### When is the UK Spring Statement 2026?

The Spring Forecast is scheduled for **3 March 2026**, with the government expected to respond by statement to Parliament on the day.

### Will taxes change in the Spring Statement?

Possibly. The Spring Forecast is expected to be an interim update rather than a full fiscal event, but changes can still happen. Recent spring measures have included NI rate cuts and threshold changes.

### Does the Spring Statement affect the new tax year?

Yes. Any changes announced are usually effective from 6 April 2026, the start of the new tax year. Some measures may take effect immediately.

### How can I prepare for potential tax changes?

Know your current tax position, ensure you've used available allowances (ISA, pension), and stay informed. Major personal finance decisions can wait until after the announcement if you're concerned about changes.

### Where can I find Spring Statement updates?

We'll publish analysis on this blog as soon as the Spring Statement is delivered. The official announcements will be at gov.uk and in the Chancellor's speech to Parliament.
