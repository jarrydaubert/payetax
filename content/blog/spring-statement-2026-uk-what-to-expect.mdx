The **Spring Forecast 2026** took place on **3 March 2026**.

For PayeTax readers, the practical conclusion is straightforward: the official Spring Forecast material did **not** announce a major new personal-tax package. It was mainly an economic and fiscal forecast update, with the government continuing to frame the Budget as the main event for major tax-policy changes.

Now that the **2026 to 2027** tax year is under way, your planning should be based on current rules and the calculator's supported rates, not on old speculation about a March tax reset.

## Quick Summary

- The Spring Forecast was published on **3 March 2026**.
- The Chancellor responded to the Office for Budget Responsibility forecast in Parliament.
- GOV.UK described the Spring Forecast as an interim update on the economy and public finances.
- The Chancellor repeated the government's commitment to **one major fiscal event a year**, with major policy changes limited to the Budget.
- We found no official Spring Forecast announcement of a new PAYE, Income Tax, employee National Insurance, ISA, or pension annual allowance reset.

## Spring Forecast or Spring Statement?

People often call this kind of event a "Spring Statement". The official 2026 GOV.UK pages call it the **Spring Forecast**.

The distinction matters because the 2026 event was positioned as a forecast update, not as a second full Budget. The January date announcement said the Spring forecast would provide an interim update on the economy and public finances, with the government responding through a statement to Parliament.

## What Actually Happened?

### 1. The OBR forecast was updated

The Office for Budget Responsibility published updated economic and fiscal forecasts. The Treasury's public release highlighted inflation, borrowing, living standards, and growth forecasts.

Those macro forecasts matter for future Budgets, but they do not automatically change your payslip.

### 2. The government kept the "one major fiscal event" framing

In the official speech, the Chancellor said the government had committed to a single major fiscal event each year, limiting major policy changes to the Budget.

For individuals, that means the Spring Forecast should be read as a policy check-in unless the official documents announce a specific tax measure.

### 3. No major new personal-tax package was announced

Based on the GOV.UK speech, date announcement, and Treasury news release checked for this refresh, the Spring Forecast did not introduce a new PAYE or personal-tax package.

That means the main personal-tax story remains the rules already in force for **2026 to 2027**, plus measures announced through Budgets and later technical updates.
